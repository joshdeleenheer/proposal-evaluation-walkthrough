Industrial Proposal Evaluation Framework

Evaluating a Proposal on Your Territory

A step-by-step walkthrough to help your Nation weigh an industrial proposal, see where it stands against best practice, and decide what to ask for.

When a company proposes a project — a mine, a pipeline, an energy facility — on your territory, the decisions come fast and the stakes are long. This tool walks you through the questions that matter most, in plain language. At each step you choose what fits your situation, and the tool shows you the strongest option alongside alternatives that may suit your circumstances better.

First your goals, then four separate relationships

Before weighing any single piece, you start by setting your Nation's own goals — what a good outcome looks like to you. Then a proposal is checked across four tracks that run in parallel, each a different relationship. One cannot stand in for another, and you can work through them in any order.

The Crown — Provincial & Federal
Government's duties to you

Canada and BC owe your Nation duties that exist whether or not you ever sign a deal with the company: to consult and seek your consent, to share government revenue, to assess the build-up of all development, and to make sure the site is cleaned up. Provincial and federal obligations are split into their own tracks.

Early agreement & the Company IBA
Your agreements with the proponent

An early agreement (MOU) can set the ground rules before the proponent acts; the IBA is the private contract over jobs, contracts, money, equity, and project-specific protections. Valuable — but not consent, no replacement for government revenue sharing, and no fix for cumulative effects on its own.

You begin with a short goals step, then choose tracks from a hub in whatever order suits you — and new tracks can be added as the research grows.

At the end you get a shareable summary — a plain-language snapshot of where the proposal stands across the tracks, scored against the goals you set, and a clear list of what to ask for, and of whom. You can print it or save it to bring into the room.
